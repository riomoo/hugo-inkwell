Veth's house was at the top of the city, where the towers were tallest and the sea-glass windows burned brightest.

Sael followed her through streets she could not quite hold in her memory — she would turn a corner and the last three turnings would blur, not forgotten exactly, but *used up*, like trying to remember a word you've said too many times. The city seemed to notice this. The dead gave her a wider berth than they gave each other. Children stopped to stare.

"They're not used to *weight*," Veth said without turning. "The living have it. We shed it, gradually, after. Give it ten years and you pass through walls without thinking."

"I won't be here ten years."

"No," Veth agreed. "You won't."

{{< scene-break >}}

The house was full of maps.

They were pinned to every wall, rolled in every corner, stacked on tables that bowed under their weight — except that the maps themselves weighed nothing, and the tables were solid, and the bowing was something the house did out of habit. Sael recognized none of the coastlines. She recognized none of the scripts. But in the center of the largest map — spread across the floor, its edges weighted with fragments of drowned stone — she recognized a shape.

The Meridian.

The sea-road between worlds. The path that cartographers drew in pencil and then erased. The thing her mother had spent thirty years looking for and never found.

"You know it," Veth said. She was watching her with those too-deep eyes.

"My mother drew that shape." Sael crouched at the map's edge. The ink was still dark, still fresh-looking, though the paper had the texture of something older than paper should be. "She called it a myth."

"All true things start as myths. The Meridian is a door." Veth moved to the far side of the map and crouched opposite her. Between them, the coastlines of impossible places curved and crossed. "Aehr drowned because we found it and tried to open it from the wrong side. Three hundred years ago, we paid for that." She looked up. "The door is still there. The question is whether anyone will ever open it *correctly*."

Sael looked at the map. She looked at the city outside the window, burning quietly in its impossible way, full of people who had given up on weight and warmth and wet footprints.

"What do you need from me?" she asked.

"To carry something." Veth stood. She reached into her coat — into somewhere *inside* her coat that seemed deeper than a coat should be — and drew out a small glass bottle. It was sealed with black wax. Inside it, something moved: pale, liquid, and faintly luminous. "The last tide of Aehr. The moment before we drowned."

Sael looked at the bottle. The thing inside it pressed against the glass.

"Where do I carry it *to?*"

"To the door." Veth set the bottle on the map, at the center, where the Meridian curved. "And then you open it."

"And then?"

Veth was quiet for a moment. Outside, the city burned on in its strange preserved way. The sea lay flat and still around it. The sky didn't move.

"And then," she said, "something drowned learns to breathe again."
